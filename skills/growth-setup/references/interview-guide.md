# Onboarding Interview Guide

The interview is the heart of setup: it must surface who the user is, what they want their brand to be, and what they want to stand out as. Batch questions (multiple-choice where possible), max 3–4 rounds. If research already answered something, confirm it instead of re-asking. Record non-answers as `TBD` and move on.

## Round 1 — Who you are

1. What do you build, write, sell, or do? (open — get concrete nouns: projects, products, services, topics)
2. What's your strongest skill or unfair advantage — the thing people already come to you for?
3. Where does your work live publicly? (site, GitHub, Substack, X, LinkedIn, YouTube, Etsy — collect links)
4. How far along are you? (just starting / some public work / established but unfocused / established and focused)

## Round 2 — What you want to stand out as

5. Finish this sentence: "I want to be the person people think of for ___."
6. Who should your work attract? (employers, clients, readers, collaborators, buyers, community)
7. If someone described you to a friend in one sentence, what do you *wish* they'd say?
8. Which existing figures or brands feel close to what you want — and what would you do differently?

## Round 3 — Brand boundaries

9. Preferred public tone (plain / weird / technical / funny / warm / anti-corporate — pick or describe).
10. What do you refuse to become? (fake guru, hype merchant, engagement farmer, corporate thought leader...) — record as hard anti-positioning constraints.
11. Topics or claims that are off-limits, even if they'd grow faster.

## Round 4 — Goals & capacity

12. Primary goal right now: get hired / grow audience / launch product / monetize existing work / explore. Plus a target and timeframe (e.g., "1k subscribers by December").
13. Hours/week you can realistically invest; rough budget.
14. Hard constraints plans must respect: work status, location, energy, health, family.
15. Opportunity types you prefer or rule out (SaaS, content, services, directories; no enterprise sales, no paid ads...).
16. Anything you've tried that failed or burned you out? (seeds the playbook)

## Handling answers

- Vague answer → reflect back a sharper version and ask for a yes/no.
- Contradiction with research → surface it gently; user decides.
- Aspirational claims → keep, but mark aspirational vs proven so positioning stays truthful.

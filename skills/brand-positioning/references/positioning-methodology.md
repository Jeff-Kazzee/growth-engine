# Positioning Methodology

## Core questions (answer before building assets)

1. Strongest truthful pattern across the work? 2. What is the user actually building toward? 3. What do they understand that peers miss? 4. Which audience finds the work unusually useful? 5. What are they credible enough to claim now? 6. Not yet credible to claim? 7. What proof would strengthen positioning most? 8. Which phrases make them generic? 9. Which are memorable but real? 10. What should they refuse to become?

## The seven filters (every option must pass)

- **Truth** — supported by real work, writing, projects, or credible trajectory?
- **Specificity** — could 10,000 other AI people say the same thing? Sharpen until no.
- **Proof** — can they point to artifacts supporting it?
- **Audience** — would the target audience immediately get why they should care?
- **Memory** — could someone describe them to a third person?
- **Anti-cringe** — does it smell like guru sludge, engagement bait, or startup cosplay?
- **Expansion** — can it grow over 6–18 months?

## Analysis sequence

### 1. Raw material inventory
Extract from profile + public work: skills, projects, themes, obsessions, values, contrarian beliefs, proof assets, audience signals, career goals, distinctive language, story-shaping constraints.

### 2. Pattern diagnosis
Name the deeper pattern underneath the work (e.g., "builder of weird useful AI-native tools", "anti-slop technical creator"). Create fresh ones from the actual context — never copy examples.

### 3. Positioning options (5–8)
```markdown
## Option: [Name]
**Core idea:** · **Best audience:** · **What this lets me claim:**
**Proof supporting:** · **Proof missing:** · **Why memorable:** · **Risks:**
**Verdict:** Keep / Modify / Reject
```
Reject weak options honestly.

### 4. Best recommendation
Why strongest, what it emphasizes/avoids, who it attracts, what proof it needs next, language to use, language to avoid.

## Deliverables

A. **Core positioning statement** — plain, memorable, and professional versions.
B. **One-line identity** — 10 bio-length one-liners.
C. **Bio variants** — X, Bluesky, GitHub, LinkedIn, Substack, Reddit, personal site, speaker intro, email signature, short about-me. Fit each platform; vary the tone.
D. **Known-for map** — primary/secondary known-for, proof to show first, topics to own / touch lightly / avoid, projects that reinforce vs confuse the signal.
E. **Project theme map** — cluster projects into 3–6 public themes: name, contents, why it matters, examples, content angles, proof needed.
F. **Content pillars (4–6)** — core promise, audience, post/essay/project types, recurring series ideas, what to avoid.
G. **Phrase bank** — 20 short phrases, 10 taglines, 10 project-category names, 10 series names, 10 "what I do" phrases. Fun not cringe, weird but understandable, specific, no hype, no fake credentials.
H. **Anti-positioning** — what they refuse to become; claims not to make yet; words to avoid; content and audience traps; trends not to chase; how to stay credible while weird.
I. **Proof-asset plan** — `| Proof asset | Purpose | Format | Difficulty | Impact | First step |` (case studies, README overhauls, demos, essays, launch pages, build logs, before/after stories, start-here pages).
J. **Profile rewrite pack** — per platform: goal, bio, header/tagline, pinned post idea, link priority, proof to show first.
K. **30-day execution plan** — Week 1 Clarify / Week 2 Build proof / Week 3 Publish / Week 4 Reinforce, concrete actions only.
L. **Brand rules** — 10 rules protecting the positioning from drifting into generic AI sludge.
